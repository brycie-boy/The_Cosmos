- Evergreen notes should be atomic

- It‚Äôs best to create notes which are only about one thing‚Äîbut which, as much as possible, capture the entirety of that thing.

- This way, it‚Äôs easier to form connections across topics and contexts. - A ‚Äúconcept handle‚Äù is a memorable noun phrase representing a complex, often abstract topic. For example: ‚Äúprisoner‚Äôs dilemma,‚Äù ‚ÄúOverton window,‚Äù ‚Äúbelief in belief,‚Äù

- When writing Evergreen notes it‚Äôs tempting to write notes like ‚ÄúX is bad‚Äù or ‚ÄúY doesn‚Äôt work.‚Äù Prefer instead to write note titles which express the property or requirement in a positive sense.

- Evergreen notes should be concept-oriented

- It‚Äôs best to factor Evergreen notes by concept (rather than by author, book, event, project, topic, etc). This way, you discover connections across books and domains as you update and link to the note over time

- The most straightforward way to take notes is to start a new note for each book, each project, or each research topic. Because each note covers many concepts, it can be hard to find what you‚Äôve written when a concept comes up again later: you have to remember the name of each book or project which dealt with the topic

- When you read another book which discusses the same concept, you‚Äôll write a new note on that book. With this approach, there‚Äôs no accumulation

- Your new thoughts on the concept don‚Äôt combine with the old ones to form a stronger whole: you just have a scattered set of notes on the concept,

- it‚Äôs not just about accumulation. There‚Äôs also no pressure to synthesize your new ideas on the concept with your prior thoughts about it.

- novel connections tend to appear where they‚Äôre not quite so expected. When arranging notes by concept, you may make surprising links between ideas that came up in very different books. You might never have noticed that those books were related before

- Organizing by concept makes note-taking a little harder, but in a useful way: when writing new notes, we have to find where they fit into the whole. So we explore some part of our prior web of notes, which may lead us somewhere unexpected.

- Prefer associative ontologies to hierarchical taxonomies

- Our file systems, organizational structures, and libraries suggest that hierarchical categories are the natural structure of the world. But often items belong in many places. And items relate to other items in very different hierarchical categories.

- once you can see the shape, then you can think about its character.

- Close open loops

- Tasks left undone, observations left unrecorded, replies yet to be written‚Äîthese swirl about our minds, as if we‚Äôre rehearsing them over and over again to make sure they‚Äôre not forgotten. - For instance: for operational to-dos, this means (Allen, 2015):

2.  You regularly drain tasks from this list
3.  You regularly delegate, refactor, or delete tasks which you can‚Äôt prioritize

- Taken together, these properties ensure that when you record a task, _you can stop thinking about it._ Ubiquitous capture isn‚Äôt enough, as most to-do systems demonstrate. - Use notes to avoid preconceived conclusions ‚ùóÔ∏è

- When writing manuscripts, one often begins with a conclusion (or at least an angle) in mind, and then we write or do research with an eye to supporting that idea. - The linear process promoted by most study guides, which insanely starts with the decision on the hypothesis or the topic to write about, is a sure-fire way to let confirmation bias run rampant.
